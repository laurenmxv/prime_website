Altamonte Springs Landscaping Considerations

As one of Seminole County's most established cities, Altamonte Springs has a mature landscape character that comes with its own set of maintenance realities.

Altamonte Springs' residential neighborhoods — particularly Sanlando Springs, Forest City, and Springs Landing — were developed primarily in the 1970s and 1980s. This means the trees and shrubs on these properties have had 40-plus years to mature. Large live oaks, slash pines, and ornamental trees create significant canopy, shade conditions, and surface root challenges that newer properties don't face. Mowing around surface roots, pruning for clearance and health, and selecting shade-tolerant plants for bed renovations all require a crew with hands-on experience, not just a crew with equipment.

The commercial corridor along SR-436 spans a wide range of property types — big-box retail with large parking lot islands, strip centers with narrow foundation beds, office parks with entry drives and lawn panels, and the Altamonte Mall complex itself. Commercial landscaping on this corridor is high-visibility work, and we approach it with a commercial-grade maintenance program that keeps properties looking managed on every visit.

Crane's Roost Park and the surrounding Cranes Roost Boulevard office district are focal points of the city. The park hosts major community events and is visible from I-4 — the surrounding commercial properties benefit from the city's investment in that corridor and need grounds that match it.

Altamonte Springs' condo and townhome communities are an active segment of the market here. Many mid-density HOA communities were built in the 1980s-1990s and have ongoing maintenance needs for aging irrigation systems, beds that need renovation, and turf that needs refreshing. We serve these associations with comprehensive programs that address both routine maintenance and longer-term landscape health.
